Stoner & Blanc (2010) asked whether the neurophysiological and performance biases found with the [Valdés-Sosa et al. (2000)](https://doi.org/10.1037/0096-1523.26.2.488) design could be explained by the biased-competition account of stimulus selection ([Desimone & Duncan, 1995](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev.ne.18.030195.001205)). According to this account, when multiple stimuli appear in the visual field they activate distinct populations of neurons that compete with each other. Which neurons win this competition can be biased by the strength of the stimuli (such as that dictated by luminance contrast) as well as by endogenously-directed selective attention ([Luck, Chelazzi, Hillyard, & Desimone, 1997](https://doi.org/10.1152/jn.1997.77.1.24); [Moran & Desimone, 1985](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.4023713); and [Reynolds, Chelazzi, & Desimone, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.19-05-01736.1999)).

Stoner & Blanc (2010) adapted the competition model of [Reynolds et al. (1999)](https://doi.org/10.1523/JNEUROSCI.19-05-01736.1999). In their application, a model neuron responding to the translation in the delayed-onset variation of the Valdés-Sosa et al. design ([Figure 3](#figure-3)) is suppressed by neurons responding to the surviving rotation (see [Stoner & Blanc, 2010](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.visres.2009.11.015), and below for further explanation).
