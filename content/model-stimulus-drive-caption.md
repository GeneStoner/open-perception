**Population stimulus drive — the input shared by both models.** The three stimulus components are represented as activity across a population of direction-tuned neurons (von Mises tuning, κ = 2) over the trial. Why can two *rotations* and a *translation* share a single motion-direction axis? Because MT neurons have large receptive fields that take in both transparent surfaces at once (Stoner & Blanc, 2010, Fig. 8A): for a receptive field positioned off the fixation point, a rigidly rotating field is *locally* approximately a translation — so the two counter-rotating fields appear as opposite translations (here **up** and **down**), while the brief coherent translation is a third direction (**right**). The model therefore receives all three as directions on one common axis. The delayed field's drive begins ≈ 750 ms in; the translation appears as the brief vertical band (≈ 1050–1090 ms). We build this drive once because the *same* input feeds the motion-competition detector and, later, the Reynolds–Heeger normalization stage. The crucial point: **A and B are identical, as are C and D** — the motion swap reproduces the *opposite* cue's no-swap drive, adding no new directional information. Since the model sees only this drive, it cannot tell a swap trial from the matched no-swap trial, and is forced to predict that the cued advantage reverses under a motion swap.
