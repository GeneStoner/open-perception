Visual attention is believed to operate at multiple levels. *Spatial attention* selects information based on location (“where”), *feature-based attention* enhances specific visual features such as color or motion direction (“what”), and object-based attention selectively enhances information belonging to coherent perceptual objects, even when those objects overlap spatially and share visual features.  Whereas spatial and feature-based attention are  accomodated by models that take advantage of the established spatial and feature-specific tuning of neurons within visual cortical areas, the mechanisms underlying supporting object-based attention remain unknown.

Visual attention is often described as operating at multiple levels. Attention can be directed to a location in space (“where”), to a visual feature such as color or motion direction (“what”), or to an object or surface as a coherent perceptual entity. While spatial and feature-based attention are relatively easy to reconcile with the known organization of visual cortex, the mechanisms that allow selective processing of whole objects remain less well understood.

Transparent-motion stimuli provide a powerful way to study this problem. In these displays, two moving dot patterns are superimposed in the same region of space, yet observers perceive two distinct moving surfaces. Because the surfaces occupy the same locations, coarse spatial selection cannot easily explain selective processing of one surface over the other. Likewise, in the experiments below, the direction of the critical translation is unpredictable, making a simple feature-based account difficult.

In the delayed-onset paradigm developed by Reynolds, Stoner and colleagues, one of the two surfaces appears slightly later than the other. A brief translation is then applied to one of the surfaces, and observers report its direction. Performance is consistently better when the translation occurs on the delayed surface. This finding suggests that the visual system can selectively process one perceptual surface and maintain that selection even as new and unpredictable motion signals emerge.
