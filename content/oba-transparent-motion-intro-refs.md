## Wertheimer, M. (1923)
**Untersuchungen zur Lehre von der Gestalt. *Psychologische Forschung*, 4, 301–350.**
**Type:** Phenomenological / psychophysical (pre-modern; observational and theoretical)
**Summary:** The foundational Gestalt paper articulating perceptual grouping principles. The *law of common fate* states that elements moving in the same direction and at the same speed group into a single perceptual object or surface — the earliest formal statement of the principle underlying global motion perception from dot fields.
**Placement:** ✓ Correctly placed — cited for common fate as the classical precursor to the Bayesian/computational parsimony claim.
**Note:** Not PubMed indexed (German-language psychology journal) but universally cited.

## Williams, D.W. & Sekuler, R. (1984)
**Coherent global motion percepts from stochastic local motions. *Vision Research*, 24, 55–62. PMID: 6695508**
**Type:** Psychophysical
**Summary:** Showed that coherent global motion is perceived from random-dot fields in which each dot follows a random walk biased toward a mean direction. Established that the visual system integrates stochastic local signals into a stable global direction percept, robust across a wide range of dot densities. The foundational behavioral characterization of RDK coherence perception.
**Placement:** ✓ Correctly placed in the psychophysics bracket, and as the standalone citation for "perceived immediately and effortlessly."

## Newsome, W.T. & Paré, E.B. (1988)
**A selective impairment of motion perception following lesions of the middle temporal visual area (MT). *Journal of Neuroscience*, 8, 2201–2211. PMID: 3385495**
**Type:** Neurophysiological (lesion study)
**Summary:** Ibotenic acid lesions of MT in rhesus monkeys selectively and severely elevated coherence thresholds on an RDK direction task, while leaving contrast sensitivity intact. Established MT as the critical cortical locus for global motion integration from dot fields — the first causal neural evidence for the MT-motion link.
**Placement:** ✓ Corrected — moved to the neurophysiological bracket alongside Britten et al. (1992) and Born & Bradley (2005).

## Britten, K.H., Shadlen, M.N., Newsome, W.T. & Movshon, J.A. (1992)
**The analysis of visual motion: a comparison of neuronal and psychophysical performance. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 12, 4745–4767. PMID: 1464765**
**Type:** Neurophysiological + psychophysical
**Summary:** Compared single MT neuron direction sensitivity directly to the monkey's psychophysical performance on the same RDK coherence task. Individual MT neurons approached psychophysical threshold, and trial-by-trial fluctuations in MT firing predicted perceptual choices. The quantitative bridge between MT population activity and motion perception decisions.
**Placement:** ✓ Correctly placed in the neurophysiological bracket (para 1) and reused in para 2 for the MT global motion claim.

## Born, R.T. & Bradley, D.C. (2005)
**Structure and function of visual area MT. *Annual Review of Neuroscience*, 28, 157–189. PMID: 16022593**
**Type:** Review (neurophysiological)
**Summary:** Comprehensive review of MT: direction and speed tuning, center-surround organization, optic flow selectivity, depth tuning, and projections to MST. Covers both single-unit physiology and behavior. The standard reference for situating MT in the cortical motion hierarchy.
**Placement:** ✓ Correctly placed as the general neurophysiological review citation.

## Yuille, A.L. & Grzywacz, N.M. (1988)
**A computational theory for the perception of coherent visual motion. *Nature*, 333, 71–74. PMID: 3362210**
**Type:** Computational
**Summary:** Framed coherent motion perception from random-dot displays as a regularization (minimum-energy) problem. The visual system's preference for a single global velocity emerges from a cost function penalizing spatial variation in velocity — a principled, mathematical account of why the system recovers one parsimonious global direction rather than N independent local velocities. The computational grounding specific to RDK coherence.
**Placement:** ✓ Correctly placed for the parsimony / probabilistic inference claim. Also correctly reused in para 3 for the transparent-motion interpretation claim.

---

## Weiss, Y., Simoncelli, E.P. & Adelson, E.H. (2002)
**Motion illusions as optimal percepts. *Nature Neuroscience*, 5, 598–604. PMID: 12021763**
**Type:** Computational + psychophysical
**Summary:** Bayesian model of motion perception: the visual system combines noisy local motion measurements with a prior favoring slow speeds to produce optimal posterior velocity estimates. Explains a wide range of motion illusions (e.g., speed underestimation at low contrast) quantitatively without special-purpose mechanisms. The modern probabilistic-inference framing for motion parsimony, with direct psychophysical validation through illusion prediction.
**Placement:** ✓ Correctly placed as the modern Bayesian/computational grounding for the parsimony claim.

## Maunsell, J.H.R. & Van Essen, D.C. (1983)
**Functional properties of neurons in middle temporal visual area of the macaque monkey. I. Selectivity for stimulus direction, speed, and orientation. *Journal of Neurophysiology*, 49, 1127–1147. PMID: 6864242**
**Type:** Neurophysiological
**Summary:** Systematic characterization of MT neuronal response properties in macaque: strong direction selectivity, speed tuning, and orientation selectivity. Established the basic physiological profile of MT as a motion-specialized area receiving V1 input. The foundational single-unit description of MT.
**Placement:** ✓ Correctly placed for the V1→MT hierarchy claim in para 2.

## Albright, T.D. (1984)
**Direction and orientation selectivity of neurons in visual area MT of the macaque. *Journal of Neurophysiology*, 52, 1106–1130. PMID: 6520628**
**Type:** Neurophysiological
**Summary:** Detailed mapping of direction and orientation selectivity across MT. Showed that MT neurons are strongly direction-selective and that direction columns are organized in a systematic columnar architecture. Complementary to Maunsell & Van Essen (1983) in establishing MT's role as the cortical hub for motion direction processing.
**Placement:** ✓ Correctly placed for the MT global motion claim in para 2.

## Duffy, C.J. & Wurtz, R.H. (1991)
**Sensitivity of MST neurons to optic flow stimuli. I. A continuum of response selectivity to large-field stimuli. *Journal of Neurophysiology*, 65, 1329–1345. PMID: 1875243**
**Type:** Neurophysiological
**Summary:** Showed that MST neurons respond selectively to large-field optic flow patterns — expansion, contraction, rotation, and translation — providing the physiological basis for heading and self-motion perception. Established MST as the stage downstream of MT that encodes complex flow fields relevant to navigation.
**Placement:** ✓ Correctly placed for the MST complex flow fields claim in para 2.

## Tanaka, K. & Saito, H. (1989)
**Analysis of motion of the visual field by direction, expansion/contraction, and rotation cells clustered in the dorsal part of the medial superior temporal area of the macaque monkey. *Journal of Neurophysiology*, 62, 626–641. PMID: 2769351**
**Type:** Neurophysiological
**Summary:** Identified distinct cell types in dorsal MST (MSTd) tuned to expansion/contraction, rotation, and translation of large visual flow fields. Complementary to Duffy & Wurtz (1991) in establishing the optic-flow selectivity of MST. Together these papers define the physiological basis for the MST-level flow field representations described in the text.
**Placement:** ✓ Correctly placed for the MST complex flow fields claim in para 2.

## Adelson, E.H. & Bergen, J.R. (1985)
**Spatiotemporal energy models for the perception of motion. *Journal of the Optical Society of America A*, 2, 284–299. PMID: 3973762**
**Type:** Computational
**Summary:** Proposed that motion is detected by spatiotemporal energy filters tuned to oriented structure in (x, y, t) space — velocity equals spatiotemporal orientation. The foundational computational model of local motion detection, corresponding to V1-level processing. Explains how individual dot motions are encoded before global integration.
**Placement:** ✓ Added to the computational bracket in para 1.

## Simoncelli, E.P. & Heeger, D.J. (1998)
**A model of neuronal responses in visual area MT. *Vision Research*, 38, 743–761. PMID: 9604103**
**Type:** Computational
**Summary:** Computational model of how MT integrates V1 direction-selective outputs to represent global pattern motion. Two-stage architecture: V1 spatiotemporal energy detectors feed into MT pattern-motion units via divisive normalization. The standard computational account of the V1→MT integration step described in the text.
**Placement:** ✓ Added to the computational bracket in para 1.

## Qian, N., Andersen, R.A. & Adelson, E.H. (1994, Part I)
**Transparent motion perception as detection of unbalanced motion signals. I. Psychophysics. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 14, 7357–7366. PMID: 7996181**
**Type:** Psychophysical
**Summary:** Random-dot displays with two opposing motion directions are perceived as transparent only when local directional signals are spatially unbalanced. Perfectly balanced opposing signals produce a non-transparent flickering percept. The key psychophysical characterization of when transparent random-dot motion is and is not perceived.
**Placement:** ✓ Cited in para 3 for the psychophysical characterization of the transparent-motion percept.

---

## Qian, N. & Andersen, R.A. (1994, Part II)
**Transparent motion perception as detection of unbalanced motion signals. II. Physiology. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 14, 7367–7380. PMID: 7996182**
**Type:** Neurophysiological
**Summary:** MT neurons are suppressed when two opposing motion directions are simultaneously present in their receptive field. The suppression underlies surface segregation: unbalanced signals allow one direction to dominate locally, driving surface-selective MT responses.
**Placement:** ✓ Cited in para 3 for the neurophysiological characterization.

---

## Qian, N., Andersen, R.A. & Adelson, E.H. (1994, Part III)
**Transparent motion perception as detection of unbalanced motion signals. III. Modeling. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 14, 7381–7392. PMID: 7996183**
**Type:** Computational
**Summary:** Computational model linking the psychophysics (Part I) and physiology (Part II): a network of direction-opponent MT-like units produces transparent motion perception when and only when local motion signals are unbalanced, consistent with both the behavioral and physiological data.
**Placement:** ✓ Cited in para 3 for the computational characterization.

## Snowden, R.J., Treue, S., Erickson, R.G. & Andersen, R.A. (1991)
**The response of area MT and V1 neurons to transparent motion. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 11, 2768–2785. PMID: 1919923**
**Type:** Neurophysiological
**Summary:** MT neurons show opponent-direction suppression when two random-dot populations move in different directions within the receptive field — the direct neural substrate of transparent motion surface segregation. V1 neurons show much less suppression, placing the critical segregation mechanism at the MT level.
**Placement:** ✓ Cited in para 3 alongside Qian & Andersen (1994, Part II) for the neurophysiological characterization.
