*Random-dot stimuli* — fields of identical dots whose individual motions are locally consistent with a single global direction (e.g., rightward translation or clockwise rotation) — have been a workhorse in psychophysical (Williams & Sekuler, 1984), neurophysiological (Newsome & Paré, 1988; Britten et al., 1992; Born & Bradley, 2005), and computational (Adelson & Bergen, 1985; Simoncelli & Heeger, 1998) research. Although no single dot unambiguously specifies the global direction, global motion is perceived immediately and effortlessly (Williams & Sekuler, 1984). This capacity echoes the Gestalt principle of *common fate* — elements sharing a motion direction group perceptually into a unified surface (Wertheimer, 1923) — and has since been given rigorous computational grounding: the visual system performs a form of probabilistic inference, recovering the single global motion that most parsimoniously explains the full set of local signals (Yuille & Grzywacz, 1988; Weiss, Simoncelli & Adelson, 2002).

This has made random-dot stimuli ideal tools for understanding how visual information local in space and time is integrated into global percepts, with the visual motion system serving as a leading model for hierarchical sensory processing in the brain. This local-to-global integration unfolds across a hierarchy of cortical motion areas: direction-selective neurons in V1 extract local motions, neurons in area MT integrate these signals to represent global pattern motion (Maunsell & Van Essen, 1983; Albright, 1984; Britten et al., 1992), and neurons in area MST combine MT outputs into representations of complex flow fields such as expansion, rotation, and heading (Duffy & Wurtz, 1991; Tanaka & Saito, 1989).

*Transparent-motion stimuli* present an additional challenge to the visual system. Two random-dot patterns moving in different directions are superimposed at the same location; the typical percept is two distinct surfaces, one sliding through the other. This phenomenon has been characterized psychophysically (Qian, Andersen & Adelson, 1994, Part I), neurophysiologically (Qian & Andersen, 1994, Part II; Snowden et al., 1991), and computationally (Qian, Andersen & Adelson, 1994, Part III). These stimuli capture, in simplified form, a fundamental challenge the visual system faces: at any given location in the retinal image, more than one value of a visual attribute — such as color or direction of motion — may need to be represented simultaneously. In the case of transparent-motion stimuli, the visual system must arrive at a parsimonious interpretation of the various motions (Yuille & Grzywacz, 1988): are they consistent with myriad independent local motions, or do they reflect two distinct surfaces each moving coherently in its own direction?
