**Why the model produces a cued advantage.** The first-on field has been rotating ~750 ms longer by the time translation occurs, so its channel has adapted down (left, red), while the delayed field is still fresh and firing high (left, green). When a field briefly translates, its rotation signal drops out of the normalization pool and disinhibits the detector — and removing the *stronger* competitor disinhibits it more. Because the cued (delayed) field is the fresher, stronger competitor, cued translation yields the larger detector response (right): a **+33%** advantage, driven entirely by the adaptation asymmetry.
